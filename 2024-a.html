Who should take the responsibility for the climate change and how much they should pay for it

Who should pay for climate change is an old and unresolved question. Countries argue about who should take the blame and how much.
There's a fascinating video over here detailing the argument and giving some interesting thoughts.

As we can see from ourworldindata's data, the growth of carbon emissions in newly industrialized countries is enormous. China, for example, is currently the world's top emitter, with annual emissions of 11.4 billion tons of carbon dioxide. While older industrialized countries, such as the United States, the United Kingdom, and parts of Europe. There are many, many different factors involved, such as the accelerated de-industrialization of Europe due to the Russia-Ukraine conflict, and the development of newer and better new energy sources. Seems like China needs to pay the biggest share of the blame ... Is it?

The main factor when considering which country is a developed country is GDP per capita, and how can we not consider per capita when considering carbon emissions? Developed countries generally have more assets as well as more enjoyment. For example, the US has a well-developed aviation industry, etc., which is supposed to be a big source of pollution. But the important point is that these countries have so few people compared to developing countries. This lowers the total emissions of the whole country. In this table, we can see that Qatar, with a very low population, has a staggering 37.6 tons of emissions per capita in 2022, and the United States has nearly 15 tons per capita, while China's per capita emissions are only a mere 8 tons. Of course, if only consider the emissions per capita, this article would be the same as those that only discuss aggregate.

First of all, if we only consider per capita, then it is not equal for those small countries, because even a small country needs to maintain a normal government and other institutions, as well as carry out some development. These carbon emissions do not grow linearly, there is a base cost. The smaller the population the larger this percentage will be.

The second thing is that talking only about per capita is also hugely unequal for the big manufacturing countries. The factories of the world provide the products that the world needs, but those carbon emissions are counted in the hands of the producing countries. From 中国进出13贸易中的隐含碳估算 paper, the data shows there are nearly 600 million tons of Embodied Carbon emmisions in the trade of China in 2006. It's nearly 9.2% of the annual carbon emissions of China wchich shows the global industrial cooperation and transfers had given certain media outlets some good leverage to demand that developing countries take on too much responsibility.

Finally, it is useless to talk only about today's carbon emissions. Developed countries had already undergone industrialization, and their past carbon emissions were counted. Those emissions have also contributed to current climate change and they have profited from it. They are now able to utilize their post-industrial development to feed themselves and even make new energy reforms. But the problem most developing countries now face is to get rich first. That was why the problem of development had to be solved by development. The developed countries have already experienced economic development, so they can begin to develop the cause of environmental protection. The developing countries, on the other hand, are currently facing the real problems of not having enough food to eat and not having warm clothes to wear. The most important thing for them is to develop themselves first and then solve their environmental problems through the benefits of development. In the final analysis, the purpose of environmental protection is to protect the environment on which human beings depend for their survival. In the end, it is all about being responsible for people. What is the point of discussing environmental protection if people are dying?

Looking at the historical totals chart we can see that the UK, which has never been on the list before, shows up, because the UK doesn't need to do a lot of emissions now through it's previous development. Moreover, the developed countries transfer the high polluting, low value-added, and labor-intensive industries to the developing countries. This shifts the responsibility from them.

But these are just arguments. There is no point in arguing. In times of crisis, only responsibility remains. So let's see how clean energy is developing in China, the world's largest emitter by far. "China is currently outpacing the 3x objective, while other countries are lagging behind. "(Page 42, global-energy-climate-trends-2024-edition-enerdata. )

Overall, climate change is a problem that needs to be addressed by all of humanity. It is not the responsibility of one country or a few countries. In this, developing countries can pay more attention to environmental protection in their development, while developed countries, because of their previous carbon emissions, need to develop better technology for environmental protection as well as to assist developing countries in their energy transition and industrialization. Developing countries could utilize technology to reduce carbon emissions from development. None of that could be done by one or two countries, but was the common responsibility of all mankind.
