Three Things You Need to Know About Bioproducts

Fermentation is a critical method for the production of Bioproducts. The process of preparing the microbial organism itself, or its direct or secondary metabolites, using the vital activities of microorganisms under aerobic or anaerobic conditions is known as fermentation. Today it is widely used in the food, biological, and chemical industries. With a certain degree of genetic modification, these microorganisms can create all sorts of strange and even completely new substances. Ethanol, biodiesel, etc. are very common among the bioproducts produced by fermentation. (lesson19)

Before fermentation can take place to make ethanol, we need to convert the raw materials into sugars that enable the yeast to produce ethanol efficiently. These sugars are in turn extracted directly from crops or made through operations such as saccharification of starch. (Ethanol_fermentation)

Of course, the output of the crop is not just starch, but also cellulose. Starch is divided into branched starch and straight chain starch, the difference between these two types of starch is that straight chain starch is a whole chain whereas branched starch has a branch-like structure. Indica rice has less branched starch than Japonica rice, so indica rice is not as sticky as Japonica rice. Another stuff that is also made up of alpha-D-glucose is glycogen, a structure generally used by animals to store sugar. This structure has even more branches than branched starch. Cellulose, which is made from β-D-glucose, is not branched which is like straight-chain starch, but because of the structure of β-D-glucose, every two glucose need to be flipped over to combine so they don't form a helical structure like α-D-glucose does. Cellulose forms hydrogen bonds between them so they are more tightly bound and harder to break apart. Bioplastics, on the other hand, are a type of bioproducts, of which classifications include bio-based plastics, degradable plastics, compostable plastics, and so on. Compostable plastics are very effective in reducing plastic waste in landfills and oceans. Previously, biodegradable plastics required specific bacteria and temperatures to degrade. Compostable plastics, on the other hand, degrade directly in the compost pile, which is more in line with the definition of “biodegradable”. This way, even if people throw away their trash bags, they don't have to deal with the problem of non-degradation. This sounds like a very good plan, but there are still some unanswered questions. For example, the cost of compostable plastics is higher than the cost of biodegradable plastics, which is much higher than regular plastics. Whether merchants and customers are willing to pay that cost is a big question. Even with the push for reusable trash bags, I forget to bring them when I use them in the real world, let alone compostable plastic bags that cost more money each time I buy them. Biodegradable plastics interfere with today's recycling systems for other plastics, so they require special recycling processes and need to be sorted. This is a huge problem, not only economically but also in terms of policy. This type of plastic is a good thing, but there are still many problems and many limitations. (lesson20)

The idea of bio-based products is a good one, but these products more or less have consequences such as increased costs and increased limitations of use. The pros and cons need to be weighed against their use and adoption by society.
