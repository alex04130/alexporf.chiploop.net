Biorenewable Resources

All feedstocks, biorenewables included, have to be sourced from somewhere

Most of the things we come into contact with daily are industrial products that have been processed, some of the things are primarily processed products, and a few of the things are raw materials. But so-called raw materials still have a source.

For example, there is a water pipe, which is made of plastic. There are several types of plastics, including natural plastics, bioplastics, and synthetic plastics. Both natural and bioplastics are derived from biological sources, mainly plants. Synthetic plastics originate from the petrochemical industry, where organic raw materials for making materials such as plastic resins and fibers are extracted from crude oil through cracking and catalysis. Crude oil is extracted from land or sea through oil wells. The reason for the formation of these crude oils remains a mystery, with some people supporting the inorganic oil formation theory and others supporting the organic oil formation theory. But none of these have an impact on tracing the sources. Organic oil formation is also divided into several types, but generally, they are formed from the bodies of living organisms, the difference lies in the period and method. The source of the living organisms contains the elements of the earth and the energy of the sun. And all the elements of the earth and the sun also come from nuclear reactions such as hydrogen fusion ................... So this kind of traceability can be endless, which explains the title: All feedstocks, bio renewables included, have to be sourced from somewhere!

Plastic water pipes are just one example which close to normal life, and there are many more such as paper, books, pencils, and so on. An important part of these raw materials are biomaterials, which are basically derived from plant growth processes. The energy from the sun's nuclear fusion is released to the earth in the form of light and is received by these plants (lesson 7). The chloroplasts within the plant use this energy to produce sugars (e.g. glucose) from the carbon dioxide collected in the air and the water absorbed by the roots. These sugars are used to make alcohol, vinegar, food, feed, and other human needs.

Some of the sugars produced by photosynthesis are used to give plants energy to grow. The formation layer in the tree grounds this energy as well as other elements absorbed by the roots for cellular replication. Through these processes of replication, the xylem of the tree is produced, which is the source of wood (lesson 7).

Herbaceous plants are richer in products than woody plants, which are primarily used as structural materials. Cellulose, a fiber-like biomaterial, is flexible and malleable enough to be used as a material for clothing, fabrics, paper, and other products. Global starch production exceeds 50 million tons per year, mainly from corn, rice, and potatoes (LRashwan, 1). This starch is mainly derived from the edible parts of plants such as fruits, roots, and stalks (i.e. food reserves for ourselves and the next generation). Some of this food is eaten by us, some is used in industry, such as biofuels, and a large part is given to livestock. The meat grown by these animals is then supplied to humans. Of course, there are products of both herbaceous and woody plants, such as oils, but they are still mainly based on soybeans, canola, and so on.

When talking about growing plants, there are questions about organic, eco-friendly, and something else like that immediately come up. According to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, more than 700 million people still face hunger (fao). This is a major context that can not be ignored when discussing environmental protection and waste problems. Organic farming that is managed well enough is bound to be more environmentally friendly than mono-crop conventional farming that focuses only on yield. Conventional agro-forestry, with its mono-cropping, the need for chemical fertilizers and pesticides and other environmentally unfriendly things, and the blow to biodiversity, are all problems. But in order to feed everyone, these are the measures that have to be used at this moment. Going completely organic may indeed lead to completely sustainable development, this contributes to the question for all, whether or not to gamble all of humanity or even give up some of it now for the sake of nebulous expectations of sustainability in the future.

Environmental protection is a necessary thing and people's lives have to be good enough too. Then reducing waste and better distribution is a part of what needs to be done. Gradually replacing traditional agriculture with environmentally friendly agriculture and enhancing the efficiency of utilization may be a better option that can be obtained at present. Hope someone will come up with a solution that will be good enough to address these problems.
